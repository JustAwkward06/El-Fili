Act by Act

Plot Summary

The story in six movements — enough to narrate confidently without re-reading all 39 chapters

Exam Angle LEPT items often test whether you know Simoun = Ibarra and the 13-year time gap between the two novels. Lead with that whenever you summarize the plot.
01 · Opening
The Steamer Tabo
The novel opens on a steamship sailing toward Laguna de Bay — a literal class divide, with friars, officials, and the wealthy on the upper deck, and ordinary Filipinos below. Simoun, a wealthy, mysterious jeweler close to the Captain-General, is introduced here. He is secretly Crisóstomo Ibarra, returned after 13 years to seek revenge.
02 · The Reveal
Simoun and Basilio
Basilio, now a medical student about to graduate, recognizes Simoun as the stranger who once helped him bury his mother Sisa. Simoun reveals his true identity and his plan for revolution — but Basilio, focused on his studies and his sweetheart Juli, declines to join.
03 · The Cause
The Students' Petition
Basilio and Isagani, along with Makaraig and other students, push for a Spanish-language academy for Filipinos. Officials like Don Custodio stall and obstruct it with bureaucratic excuses, exposing the hollowness of colonial "reform."
04 · Escalation
Simoun Fuels the Fire
Simoun deliberately worsens corruption and suffering — manipulating officials, exploiting land disputes like Kabesang Tales's, and pushing the population toward breaking point — believing only desperation will spark revolt.
05 · The Plot
The Wedding Bomb
Simoun plans to detonate a lamp rigged with explosives at Paulita Gómez and Juanito Peláez's wedding, intending to kill the colonial elite in one stroke and ignite the uprising. Isagani, discovering the plot, throws the bomb into the river — saving lives but unknowingly being remembered as a thief.
06 · The End
Simoun's Death
Wounded and pursued, Simoun seeks refuge with Padre Florentino. Already grieving María Clara's death, he confesses his entire plan and philosophy before dying. Padre Florentino answers him with the novel's defining counter-argument: that freedom without moral transformation only replaces one tyranny with another.

What It's Really About

Core Themes

The angles examiners actually probe — know these cold

Theme 01
Reform vs. Revolution
The novel's central debate: can change come peacefully through education (Isagani's path), or does it require force (Simoun's path)? Rizal lets Simoun's logic feel persuasive, then has it fail.
"What is the death of a man beside the death of a society?" — Simoun
Theme 02
Hypocrisy & Clerical Abuse
Friars like Padre Salvi and Padre Camorra use religion as a tool of control and personal gain rather than genuine faith — a direct critique of how the Church propped up colonial power.
Theme 03
Education and Freedom
The students' fight for a Spanish-language academy stands in for a larger question: does access to education liberate Filipinos, or does the colonial system make sure it never truly does?
Theme 04
Moral Cost of Independence
Padre Florentino's closing argument is the novel's thesis: independence without moral transformation just swaps one oppressor for another.
"Why independence, if the slaves of today will be the tyrants of tomorrow?" — Padre Florentino
Theme 05
Greed and Corruption
The English title, The Reign of Greed, says it outright — nearly every authority figure in the novel is driven by self-interest dressed up as duty, faith, or progress.
Theme 06
The Youth as Hope — and Risk
Basilio, Isagani, and Plácido represent different youth responses to oppression: ambition, idealism, and quiet endurance. Rizal asks whether any of these paths is actually enough.
"Where are the youth who will consecrate their golden hours to the welfare of their native land?" — Padre Florentino

The Central Figures

Simounné Crisóstomo Ibarra
Revolution

A wealthy, mysterious jeweler close to the Captain-General. Beneath the disguise is Ibarra, the idealist of Noli Me Tángere, now a cold revolutionary bent on revenge.

Key Events

  • Returns in disguise, using wealth and political influence built over 13 years in exile.
  • Deliberately worsens corruption, believing suffering will trigger revolt.
  • Plots the wedding bomb — foiled by Isagani at the last second.
  • Dies confessing to Padre Florentino after María Clara's death halts his plans.
"What is the death of a man beside the death of a society?"Simoun

Represents: the revolutionary who believes force is the only real path to change — and Rizal's caution against it.

One-line recall: Simoun is Ibarra in disguise, pushing the country toward revolution through manufactured suffering.
BasilioSisa's son
Youth

A diligent medical student, raised up from poverty as Captain Tiago's protégé — the novel's moral center, trying to build something real within an unjust system.

Key Events

  • Recognizes Simoun as the stranger who helped him bury Sisa in Noli Me Tángere.
  • Refuses Simoun's first offer to join the revolution, choosing medicine and Juli instead.
  • Is eventually drawn toward Simoun's cause after personal tragedy strips away his neutrality.
"Some day you will perhaps be a great physician… but greater yet will be he who can inject a new idea into this anemic people."Simoun, recruiting Basilio

Represents: educated youth focused on personal success, slowly forced to confront the cost of staying neutral.

One-line recall: Basilio is the practical, ambitious student who only joins the cause once it becomes personal.
Isaganipoet, Florentino's nephew
Youth

Basilio's idealistic friend, raised by his uncle Padre Florentino — believes in reform through peaceful, principled means.

Key Events

  • Advocates for the Spanish-language academy, lobbying figures like Señor Pasta.
  • Turns himself in during the student crackdown out of solidarity — loses Paulita as a result.
  • Discovers and stops Simoun's wedding bomb, unrecognized as a hero.
"We don't buy jewels, because we don't need them."Isagani, to Simoun

Represents: idealistic, patriotic youth who choose conviction over violence — and pay a personal price for it.

One-line recall: Isagani is the idealist who stops the bomb and is called a thief for it.
María ClaraIbarra's former fiancée
Common Folk

Never appears directly in this novel, but remains the emotional core of Simoun's entire motivation.

Key Events

  • Entered a convent believing Ibarra dead at the end of Noli Me Tángere.
  • Dies of illness the same day Simoun's rescue plan was to begin.

Represents: purity destroyed by the system — and personal grief that ultimately outweighs political ambition.

One-line recall: María Clara's death is the emotional turning point that derails Simoun's revolution.

Church

Carried over from Noli Me Tángere as Ibarra's old enemy — continues ruling through fear and manipulation.

Church

Drill Mode

Quick-Fire Q&A

Cover the answers and test yourself before the exam

Who is Simoun in disguise?
Crisóstomo Ibarra, the protagonist of Noli Me Tángere, 13 years later.
What does "Filibustero" mean in this context?
A dangerous patriot or revolutionary — someone working against colonial rule.
What is the novel's English title and why?
"The Reign of Greed" — greed and self-interest drive nearly every authority figure in the story.
Who stops Simoun's bomb plot?
Isagani, who throws the rigged lamp into the river.
Why does Simoun abandon his final plan?
He learns of María Clara's death and is overcome by grief.
Who delivers the novel's final moral argument?
Padre Florentino, who counters Simoun's belief in violent revolution.
What is the academy the students are fighting for?
An independent Spanish-language academy for Filipino students.
Whose father is abducted by bandits?
Juli's father, Kabesang Tales.
Who is the priest who supports the students?
Padre Fernández.
To whom is the novel dedicated?
Gomez, Burgos, and Zamora — the three priests executed in 1872.

Delivery Guide

Presenting This to a Class or Panel

A suggested order if you need to report this live

Suggested Flow (8–10 min report)

  1. Open with context — title meaning, publication year, that it's Noli's sequel, 13-year time jump.
  2. Walk the plot using the six timeline beats above — don't read chapter-by-chapter, just hit the turning points.
  3. Introduce Simoun and Basilio first — they anchor the whole plot; everyone else orbits them.
  4. Name 2–3 themes and tie each to one quote — examiners and classmates remember quotes, not summaries.
  5. Close with Florentino's question — "why independence, if the slaves of today will be the tyrants of tomorrow?" It's the strongest line to end on.
Reminder If asked to relate the novel to present-day Philippine society, the safest angle is the reform vs. revolution debate and the moral cost of power — both still come up regularly in LEPT social-relevance items.
