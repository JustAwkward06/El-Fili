The Central Figures

The man behind the plot, and the people his return puts in motion


Simoun né Crisóstomo Ibarra
Revolution

A wealthy, mysterious jeweler who has won the confidence of the Captain-General. Beneath the blue-tinted glasses and white wig is Ibarra, the idealist of Noli Me Tángere, presumed dead and now thirteen years older — and consumed by revenge.

Key Events

  • Returns to the Philippines in disguise, having used his wealth and a wartime friendship with the Captain-General to gain political influence.
  • Deliberately fuels corruption and injustice, believing that more suffering will push the people toward revolt.
  • Plots a bomb at Paulita and Juanito's wedding, meant to ignite the uprising — foiled at the last moment.
  • Abandons the plan upon learning of María Clara's death, and dies a broken man at Padre Florentino's house.
"What is the death of a man beside the death of a society?" Simoun, justifying his plan

Represents: the revolutionary who believes change must come through force — and whose downfall is Rizal's warning against revenge as a foundation for freedom.

Basilio Sisa's son
Youth

A diligent medical student on the verge of finishing his degree, raised up from poverty and tragedy as Captain Tiago's protégé. He represents hope through education — but also the danger of looking away from injustice for too long.

Key Events

  • Carries the trauma of his mother Sisa's death and his brother's disappearance from Noli Me Tángere.
  • Becomes Captain Tiago's caretaker and eventual estate manager while finishing his studies.
  • Is approached by Simoun, who tries to recruit him into the planned revolt using guilt and grief as leverage.
  • Is swept into Simoun's revolution after personal losses make neutrality impossible.
"Some day you will perhaps be a great physician… but greater yet will be he who can inject a new idea into this anemic people." Simoun, recruiting Basilio

Represents: educated Filipino youth who pursue personal success while staying disengaged from the larger struggles of their people — until reality forces their hand.

Isagani poet, nephew of Padre Florentino
Youth

Basilio's close friend and an idealistic young poet, raised by his uncle Padre Florentino. He pushes for reform through peaceful, principled means — and pays for his convictions in love as well as in politics.

Key Events

  • Advocates passionately for a Spanish-language academy for Filipino students, lobbying figures like Señor Pasta.
  • Turns himself in when students are imprisoned over rebellious pamphlets, as an act of solidarity — costing him Paulita's affection.
  • Discovers Simoun's bomb plot at Paulita's wedding and single-handedly stops it, saving everyone inside.
  • Goes unrecognized for his heroism — guests instead brand him a thief for the chaos that follows.
"We don't buy jewels, because we don't need them." Isagani, piqued by Simoun's province remark

Represents: the patriotic, principled youth who believe change is possible through conviction and peaceful means rather than violence.

María Clara Ibarra's former fiancée
Common Folk

Though she never appears directly in this novel, her presence shapes everything Simoun does. Forced into a convent after believing Ibarra dead, she remains the emotional core of his entire plan for revenge and reunion.

Key Events

  • Entered a nunnery after hearing of Ibarra's supposed death at the end of Noli Me Tángere.
  • Remains the reason Simoun returns to the Philippines, as he plans to help her escape the convent.
  • Dies of illness on the very day Simoun's rescue plan was to be set in motion — arriving too late.
  • Her death devastates Simoun and directly delays his plans for revolt.

Represents: purity and innocence destroyed by a corrupt system — and the personal grief that, in the end, proves more powerful than Simoun's political ambition.

Church & Colonial Power

The friars, officials, and elites who keep the system running


Padre Salvi
Church

A Franciscan friar carried over from Noli Me Tángere, where he was Ibarra's enemy and architect of his downfall. In this novel he continues to rule through manipulation, fear, and religious authority.

Key Events

  • Continues controlling the townspeople through religious fear and influence over Church affairs.
  • Uses manipulation freely, willing to harm others to preserve his own power and standing.

Represents: the evil and abuse the Church inflicted on ordinary Filipinos under colonial rule.

Padre Camorra
Church

The parish priest of Tiani — rough-mannered, lecherous, and openly disrespectful toward women, abusing his position for personal gratification.

Key Events

  • Pursues and abuses Juli, showing the predatory behavior some friars indulged in unchecked.
  • Frequently mocks the more liberal views of journalist Ben-Zayb, despite their otherwise similar lifestyles.

Represents: the disrespect and abuse of power foreign clergy directed at Filipinos, particularly women.

Padre Fernández
Church

A rare exception among the friars — a Dominican priest with a genuinely open mind, who actually listens to student grievances rather than dismissing them outright.

Key Events

  • Befriends Isagani and promises that he and other sympathetic priests will support the students' demands.
  • Cares about education reform in a way most of his peers do not.

Represents: the small minority within the Church capable of empathy and reform.

Captain Tiago Don Santiago de los Santos
Elite

A wealthy Manila businessman who aligns himself with friars and officials purely to protect his status — hypocrisy dressed as piety. María Clara's loss unravels him completely.

Key Events

  • Maintains influence by staying useful to priests and government officials rather than out of genuine belief.
  • Falls into depression and opium addiction after María Clara enters the convent.
  • Dies from shock upon hearing of Basilio's arrest and rumors of violent revolt.

Represents: the moral decay of those who cling to a corrupt system for comfort rather than conviction.

Don Custodio
Elite

An influential Manila politician who built business success into political capital, sitting on numerous committees while contributing little of substance.

Key Events

  • Objects to Simoun's reckless infrastructure proposal during the river trip, citing cost and damage to towns.
  • Is coldly rebuffed by Simoun, who argues great enterprises are built on the backs of forced, unpaid labor.
"Excuse me, Señor Simoun, my respected friend, if I should say that I am not of your opinion." Don Custodio, objecting to Simoun's plan

Represents: performative liberalism among the colonial elite — reform-minded in speech, self-interested in practice.

Captain Basilio
Elite

A prominent local figure, friendly with friars and government officials, who acts as an intelligent, careful mediator between the town and colonial power — always to protect his own interests.

Key Events

  • While buying jewels from Simoun, accidentally reveals deep cultural and historical ignorance shared by much of the colonial elite.

Represents: local leaders who keep the peace by aligning with power rather than challenging it.

Ben-Zayb anagram of Ibáñez
Elite

A Manila journalist with an inflated sense of his own importance, whose writing prioritizes flattering the powerful over reporting honestly.

Key Events

  • Reports negligently, more concerned with style and self-image than accuracy.
  • Trades barbs with Padre Camorra over their differing (if equally self-serving) worldviews.

Represents: a press that serves power instead of truth.

Quiroga
Elite

A foreign businessman who leverages wealth and political connections to expand his enterprises, compromising with friars and officials whenever it serves his bottom line.

Key Events

  • Maintains his business empire by staying useful to whoever holds power, regardless of the cost to Filipinos.

Represents: the spread of corruption through foreign commercial interests under colonial rule.

The Youth Movement & the People

Students, families, and the ordinary Filipinos caught between both worlds


Plácido Penitente
Youth

One of Padre Valerio's top students at Tanuan High School. His very name carries meaning — "plácido" (silent) and "penitente" (suffering) together evoke quiet, suppressed endurance.

Key Events

  • Endures the cruelty of Father Millón, his physics instructor, who delights in humiliating students with questions he never taught them to answer.

Represents: "silent suffering" — students worn down by an education system designed to break rather than build them.

Makaraig
Youth

A wealthy Filipino student and one of the leaders of the movement to establish a Spanish-language academy — privileged, but willing to put his resources behind the cause.

Key Events

  • Lends one of his own houses to serve as the schoolhouse for the planned academy.
  • Leads the student coalition pushing for educational reform.

Represents: the resourced youth who choose to use their privilege for collective good.

Juanito Peláez
Youth

A mestizo student and son of the businessman Don Timoteo — a ruthless opportunist who joins the student movement for appearances, not conviction.

Key Events

  • Abandons the movement the moment the government crackdown begins.
  • Successfully avoids punishment by distancing himself in time.

Represents: performative activism — youth who join a cause only as far as it's safe.

Juli Basilio's sweetheart
Common Folk

A simple, devoted young woman who sacrifices everything — including her own freedom — for the people she loves, after her father Kabesang Tales is abducted by bandits.

Key Events

  • Borrows ransom money from Hermana Penchang, working as her live-in servant to repay the debt.
  • Becomes a target of Padre Camorra's predatory advances, showing the dangers ordinary women faced.

Represents: ordinary Filipinos who would rather suffer with dignity than abandon family and honor.

Kabesang Tales Telesforo Juan de Dios
Common Folk

A hardworking farmer driven to desperation after losing his land to friar estates — his suffering becomes a recruiting case study for Simoun's revolution.

Key Events

  • Is abducted by bandits, forcing his daughter Juli into debt and servitude to raise ransom money.
  • His losses at the hands of friars push him toward Simoun's cause for radical change.

Represents: the common tenant farmer crushed by land-grabbing under the friar estates.

Tandang Selo
Common Folk

Father of Kabesang Tales and grandfather of Tano and Juli — an old man who once cared for the young, orphaned Basilio in Noli Me Tángere.

Key Events

  • Dies in a mountain encounter, killed by a battalion that includes his own grandson Tano among its ranks.

Represents: the tragic, almost absurd cruelty of a colonial system that turns families against each other.

Padre Florentino
Moral Voice

Isagani's uncle and a former priest — quiet, principled, and the novel's moral counterweight to Simoun. He is with Simoun at the very end, hearing his confession before his death.

Key Events

  • Shelters Simoun in his final hours and listens as the dying revolutionary recounts his failed plot.
  • Answers Simoun's philosophy of force with his own philosophy of moral transformation.
"Why independence, if the slaves of today will be the tyrants of tomorrow?" Padre Florentino, to Simoun
"Where are the youth who will consecrate their golden hours to the welfare of their native land?" Padre Florentino, near the novel's close

Represents: Rizal's own answer to Simoun — that freedom without moral renewal simply replaces one tyranny with another.

Paulita Gómez
Elite

Isagani's girlfriend, raised in privilege under her aunt's care — a relationship that ultimately steers her away from his ideals and toward comfort and status.

Key Events

  • Grows distant from Isagani after his imprisonment, eventually choosing to marry the opportunistic Juanito Peláez instead.

Represents: privileged Filipinas raised without nationalism — comfort prioritized over conviction.

Compiled from José Rizal's El Filibusterismo (1891) and its English translation, The Reign of Greed, by Charles Derbyshire.
